This side of the website

I’ll get to this, surely — but a quick explainer is that I consider this website a sandwichboard. The sandwichboard is an important metaphor for me: it is something that can be not only standalone but also worn and traveled. The sandwichboard is like a T-shirt as a billboard. (And how potent other media, wares are: yard signs, bumper stickers, hats.)

In past iterations of this website, I’ve struggled to delineate what is cleanly “work” v. “play”; I’d wanted to be able to categorize things neatly — how lovely to be able to point to something and declare, “that is me!”

I’ve since let go of this sort of thinking and embraced this tension where I am both fascinated by and frustrated with advertising. From Yedoye on Subway Takes: “Society went down a very dark path by making commercials funny. ... Because we’ve blurred the lines between what’s real and what’s not. We are now clamoring for the right to be marketed to. That is our idea of liberation.”

As a bright-eyed design student I used to exclaim “we really just wanna be artists... but don’t wanna take up the mantle of what ‘art’ entails or the responsibility that comes with it...” — that advertising is graphic design’s way of making art within the society and economic imaginaries we live in.

As a computational design student now (I think for me this label really only means that I am actively trying to engage more computer) the sandwichboard is a reminder of how we construct meaning from the environments in which we encounter resonant media. Advertising, then, is a medium through which to think through the ways we’ve been told to desire, express, navigate the world. I wouldn’t describe what I’m doing as necessarily trying to resist the at times sycophantic impulses of advertising — and by extension, technologies — but rather I hope to make “stuff” that people find useful (for conversation and reflection), entertaining, or ideally — both.

Data Entanglement (Oct 2024)

A reflection on how the data I produce flows through my local internet of things: particularly interested in the materiality of the internet (specifically, my router data), I found a 2021 Federal Trade Commission report examining how six major internet service providers (mine included) “amass large pools of sensitive data, and that their uses of such data could lead to significant harms, particularly when consumers are classified by demographic characteristics.”

Rotating the knob/dial refreshes server locations between which data packets travel: this includes the hosting service, state, and local weather.
